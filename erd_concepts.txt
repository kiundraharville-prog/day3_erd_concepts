Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) Concepts

1. How are entities defined and represented in an ERD?

Entities represent real-world objects or concepts, such as a Person, Pet, or Course.
In an ERD, entities are represented by rectangles. Each entity usually becomes a table in a database.


2. How are attributes defined and associated with entities?

Attributes are properties or characteristics of an entity.
For example, a Person entity may have attributes like name, age, and email.
In an ERD, attributes are typically shown as ovals connected to their entity.
In a database, attributes become columns in a table.


3. What is a relationship, how is it represented, and how does it connect entities?

A relationship shows how two entities are connected or interact with each other.
Relationships are represented by lines (or diamonds in some diagrams) connecting entities.
They define how data in one table relates to data in another.

Example:
A Person owns a Pet.


4. List and describe the different types of Relationships (Cardinality)

One-to-One (1:1):
One record in Entity A is related to one record in Entity B.
Example: One person has one passport.

One-to-Many (1:N):
One record in Entity A can be related to many records in Entity B.
Example: One person can own many pets.

Many-to-Many (N:M):
Many records in Entity A can be related to many records in Entity B.
Example: Students can enroll in many courses, and courses can have many students.
This type usually requires a junction table.


5. ERD: People that own Pets

Entities:
Person
Pet

Attributes:

Person:
- person_id (Primary Key)
- first_name
- last_name

Pet:
- pet_id (Primary Key)
- name
- type
- age
- person_id (Foreign Key)

Relationship:
A Person owns a Pet.

Cardinality:
One-to-Many (1:N)
One person can own multiple pets, but each pet belongs to one person.

Explanation:
The foreign key (person_id) is placed in the Pet entity to link each pet to its owner.
